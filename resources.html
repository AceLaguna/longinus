Site Disclaimer & Editorial Approach

This website — Longinus: Tip of the Spear — focuses primarily on the Biblical accounts of the spear-thrust at the Crucifixion (John 19:34) and the documented historical record of the Holy Lance relics as physical objects. These two categories form the scholarly and theological core of the site.

Extra-Biblical traditions — including the name "Longinus," his conversion legend, his martyrdom in Cappadocia, and the hagiographic narrative — are clearly labelled as later Christian tradition rather than scripture. They are presented with their historical context and documented source dates.

Modern claims about the "Spear of Destiny" — including occult power legends, the Ravenscroft narratives, and World War II mythology — are treated as legend and speculation, clearly distinguished from historical fact. No page on this site presents these claims as credible history.

All external links open in a new tab. Inclusion of a resource does not imply endorsement of all content on that site. Reliability ratings on resource cards reflect the consensus of mainstream biblical scholarship and archaeology.

Kunsthistorisches Museum — Secular Treasury (Weltliche Schatzkammer)
Kunsthistorisches Museum Wien · Vienna, Austria · khm.at

The Vienna (Hofburg) Spear is held in the Secular Treasury (Room XI) of the KHM — the most-studied Holy Lance claimant. Metallurgical analysis in 2003 dated the blade to c. 7th century AD at earliest. The museum's own collection description and the scientific report summary are available online. The lance is on permanent public display.

✦ Primary Relic Site Scientific Analysis
Reliability
↗ khm.at

Vatican — St. Peter's Basilica & Treasury
Vatican State · Rome, Italy · vaticanstate.va

The Vatican Spear is kept within a pier of St. Peter's Basilica, beneath Bernini's 1638 marble statue of Saint Longinus. Gifted to Pope Innocent VIII by Ottoman Sultan Bayezid II in 1492. The Vatican has not permitted metallurgical testing. The Fabbrica di San Pietro (the administrative body managing St. Peter's) provides visiting information and information on the crossing pier relics.

Relic Site Catholic Tradition
Reliability
↗ vaticanstate.va

Wawel Cathedral Treasury — Kraków
Wawel Royal Castle · Kraków, Poland · wawel.krakow.pl

The Wawel Cathedral holds the Kraków spear — a 14th-century copy of the Vienna spear, gifted by Holy Roman Emperor Charles IV around 1350. Acknowledged as a copy, not claimed as the original. The Treasury and Crown Rooms (Skarbiec) are open to the public. The Wawel website provides collection information and visiting details.

Holy Etchmiadzin — Armenia
Mother See of Holy Etchmiadzin · Vagharshapat, Armenia

The Armenian Apostolic Church venerates the Etchmiadzin Spear (also called the Geghard, or "Spear") as a Passion relic. The Mother See of Holy Etchmiadzin — the oldest state church in the world — holds the relic in its treasury. The monastery of Geghard (a UNESCO World Heritage Site) takes its name from this relic's association. First documented in Armenian sources in the 13th century.

Use With Caution — Popular & Speculative Sources
3 resources · Documented but unreliable or speculative

The following sources are listed because they are widely encountered when researching the Holy Lance. They are included here so readers can understand their limitations — not as recommendations. Each is annotated with specific scholarly critiques.

⚠
The Spear of Destiny — Trevor Ravenscroft (1973)
Trevor Ravenscroft · Samuel Weiser Inc., 1973 · Popular occult history

Ravenscroft's book claims that Hitler's obsession with the Vienna Spear's occult power drove his political career, and that its capture by American forces on April 30, 1945 triggered his death. The book was an international bestseller and remains widely cited in popular media.

Scholarly Critique: Ravenscroft claimed key sections were based on conversations with Walter Johannes Stein — who had died 16 years before the book's publication. Historians Nicholas Goodrick-Clarke and James Webb have documented its fabricated sourcing. No Nazi primary document corroborates Hitler's specific occult fixation on the Vienna lance. Not a reliable historical source.

Acts of Pilate (Gospel of Nicodemus) — c. 4th Century AD
Apocryphal text · Available via Early Christian Writings (earlychristianwritings.com)

The earliest text to name the spear-bearer "Longinus." Non-canonical and not accepted as scripture by any major Christian tradition. Historically significant as a document of 4th-century Christian piety and the origins of the Longinus tradition — not as a historical source for 1st-century events. The text is available in English translation at Early Christian Writings (earlychristianwritings.com).

Status: Apocryphal. Not canonical scripture. Its historical value lies in documenting when and how the Longinus tradition developed — not in its claims about 1st-century events.
