Art & Depictions
Across the Centuries

Artistic interpretations across centuries — from a 6th-century Syrian manuscript to Baroque marble, every age has reimagined the soldier, the spear, and the wound.

Manuscript · 586 AD
✦ Earliest Known

The Rabula Gospels — Crucifixion Miniature

586 AD

Unknown Syrian monk · Monastery of St. John of Zagba

The earliest surviving depiction in which the soldier is labelled "Longinus" by name — inscribed in Syriac script. This single illumination fixed his identity in Christian art for fifteen centuries. Biblioteca Medicea Laurenziana, Florence.

Wikimedia Commons Illuminated MS
Gothic · c. 1308

Crucifixion — Maestà Altarpiece

c. 1308–1311

Duccio di Buoninsegna · Siena

The defining Sienese Gothic Crucifixion, bridging Byzantine gold and early Renaissance humanity. The lance-bearer stands at right, his gesture codified by centuries of iconography.

Wikimedia Commons Tempera · Panel
Renaissance · 1441

The Crucifixion with Saints

c. 1441–1442

Fra Angelico · Convent of San Marco, Florence

Luminous chapter-house fresco painted for Dominican contemplation. Longinus stands at right with the lance, rendered with Fra Angelico's characteristic tender sincerity. In situ, Museo di San Marco.

Wikimedia Commons Fresco
Renaissance · c. 1515

Isenheim Altarpiece — Crucifixion

c. 1512–1516

Matthias Grünewald · Unterlinden Museum, Colmar

The most viscerally agonising Crucifixion in Western art — commissioned for plague-hospital patients. The lance-wound is prominent; the soldier stands in dark armour at right, the deed done.

Wikimedia Commons Oil · Limewood
Renaissance · 1565

The Crucifixion

1565

Jacopo Tintoretto · Scuola Grande di San Rocco, Venice

One of the largest oil paintings in existence. The centurion on horseback with the lance occupies lower right of a churning, storm-lit panorama of overwhelming dramatic force.

Wikimedia Commons Oil · Canvas
Baroque · c. 1620
Notable Work

Le Coup de Lance (The Lance Thrust)

c. 1620

Peter Paul Rubens · Royal Museum of Fine Arts, Antwerp

The defining Baroque image of the spear-thrust — Longinus on horseback, lance driving upward into Christ's side, face averted, blood cascading. Rubens makes the act impossible to look away from.

Wikimedia Commons Oil · Canvas
Baroque · 1632

Cristo Crucificado

c. 1632

Diego Velázquez · Museo del Prado, Madrid

A masterwork of silence. The lance-wound is present but not dramatised — the body bears it in stillness. One of the most contemplated images of the Crucifixion in Western art.

Wikimedia Commons Oil · Canvas
Baroque Sculpture · 1638
Notable Work

Saint Longinus

1635–1638

Gian Lorenzo Bernini · St. Peter's Basilica, Vatican

Colossal 4.4m marble for the crossing of St. Peter's. Arms flung wide, head thrown back — the instant of conversion made permanent in stone. Stands above the Vatican's own Holy Lance relic.

Wikimedia Commons Marble
Baroque · 1653

The Three Crosses

1653

Rembrandt van Rijn · Rijksmuseum, Amsterdam

Supreme achievement of printmaking — light from heaven pours onto the central Cross while figures dissolve into darkness. The centurion with lance is visible at right in this thunderous drypoint etching.

Wikimedia Commons Drypoint
Mannerist · c. 1590

Christ on the Cross Adored by Donors

c. 1590

El Greco · Musée du Louvre, Paris

El Greco's mystically charged, storm-lit Crucifixion shows the lance-wound clearly at Christ's side. Byzantine spirituality fused with Venetian colour, in a cosmos shaken by the event.

Wikimedia Commons Oil · Canvas
