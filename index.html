The Legend Begins

A Single Thrust. An Eternal Mystery.

On a hill outside Jerusalem, as the sun darkened and the earth trembled, a Roman soldier raised his lance and thrust it into the side of a man already dead. From that moment, the weapon became more than iron and wood — it became a conduit of destiny, sought by emperors, crusaders, mystics, and dictators across two thousand years of history.

What do we truly know? What has been embellished by faith and legend? Where is the Spear today — and does it matter?

A Summary of the Biblical Record

Separating documented scripture from later legend and mysticism

I
The Gospel Account

Only the Gospel of John (19:34) records the spear thrust. The three Synoptic Gospels — Matthew, Mark, and Luke — make no mention of it. John writes as an eyewitness: "One of the soldiers pierced his side with a spear, and immediately blood and water came out." Most scholars date this account to c. 90–110 AD.

II
The Soldier's Identity

The Gospel of John does not name the soldier. The name "Longinus" appears first in the 4th-century Acts of Pilate (Gospel of Nicodemus) — a non-canonical text. The name may derive from the Greek longche (λόγχη), simply meaning "spear," suggesting it is a titular epithet rather than a given name.

III
Medical & Theological Significance

The "blood and water" detail has been interpreted theologically as symbolic of the Eucharist and Baptism, and medically as evidence of a pleural effusion or pericardial fluid — consistent with crucifixion trauma and death. It became a central image in Christian iconography and sacramental theology.

IV
The First Known Relic Claim

The earliest historical record of a physical spear relic dates to the Pilgrim of Piacenza (c. 570 AD), who reported seeing "the lance of Our Lord" in Jerusalem's Church of the Holy Sepulchre. The relic subsequently disappeared from the historical record during the Persian conquest of Jerusalem in 614 AD.

V
The Vienna Spear: Modern Analysis

The spear held in the Kunsthistorisches Museum, Vienna — the "Hofburg Spear" — is the most studied claimant. Scientific analysis in 2003 dated its iron blade to the 7th century AD at earliest, consistent with Carolingian craftsmanship rather than Roman-era manufacture. A nail embedded in its center is of indeterminate age. It is historically significant — but almost certainly not the original lance.

NOTE:   Whether or not any surviving relic is authentic, the scriptural event itself — and the profound historical and spiritual weight it has carried for two millennia — remains one of history's most consequential single moments.
