✦   Chapter I   ✦

The Soldier
Called Longinus

A nameless act. An eternal consequence. Who was the legionary who thrust the lance — and what became of him?

Part One

The Biblical Record

What the Gospel of John actually says — and nothing more.

✦ Trusted Biblical Core

A Single Verse. One Unnamed Soldier.

Of the four canonical Gospels — Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John — only John records that a soldier pierced the side of Christ with a spear. The three Synoptic Gospels are entirely silent on the matter. The Fourth Gospel, widely dated by scholars to c. 90–110 AD, presents this detail in a resurrection and witness context: the soldier's act, unintentionally, fulfils two Hebrew prophecies simultaneously.

Critically, the soldier is given no name. He appears for three verses and vanishes. The Gospel's interest is theological — the blood and water, the unbroken bones, the fulfilment of scripture — not biographical. The man himself is, to John, incidental.

This is the irreducible historical core. Everything else — the name, the biography, the conversion, the miracles — belongs to a later tradition built atop this single, brief, unrepeated passage.

Key facts from John 19

  • It is the day of Passover preparation; the Jewish authorities ask Pilate to hasten death so bodies are not left on the cross over the Sabbath.
  • Soldiers break the legs of the two men crucified alongside Jesus — a practice called crurifragium, to accelerate suffocation.
  • Finding Jesus already dead, one soldier pierces his side with a spear (lonchē, λόγχη in Greek). Blood and water flow out.
  • John presents this as eyewitness testimony and fulfilment of Psalms 34:20 and Zechariah 12:10.
John 19:31–37 · English Standard Version

31Since it was the day of Preparation, and so that the bodies would not remain on the cross on the Sabbath (for that Sabbath was a high day), the Jews asked Pilate that their legs might be broken and that they might be taken away.

32So the soldiers came and broke the legs of the first, and of the other who had been crucified with him.

33But when they came to Jesus and saw that he was already dead, they did not break his legs.

34But one of the soldiers pierced his side with a spear, and at once blood and water came out.

35He who saw it has borne witness — his testimony is true, and he knows that he is telling the truth — that you also may believe.

36For these things took place that the Scripture might be fulfilled: "Not one of his bones will be broken."

37And again another Scripture says, "They will look on him whom they have pierced."

— John 19:31–37, ESV

Medical note

The "blood and water" has been interpreted medically as a pleural effusion or pericardial fluid separating post-mortem — consistent with death by crucifixion. It has also been read sacramentally as prefiguring the Eucharist (blood) and Baptism (water).

Part Two

The Name "Longinus"

Where it came from, when it appeared, and what it likely means.

◈ Later Christian Tradition

From Greek lonchē (λόγχη) — "spear"

The name Longinus almost certainly derives from the Greek word λόγχη (lonchē), meaning "lance" or "spear" — the very word used by John in verse 34. In other words, the soldier was likely named after his instrument, not recorded by name and later associated with a spear. The appellation is titular, not biographical. It identifies the soldier by what he did, not who he was.

First Appearance: The Gospel of Nicodemus

The name appears in writing for the first time in the Acts of Pilate, also known as the Gospel of Nicodemus — an apocryphal (non-canonical) text compiled no earlier than the 4th century AD, though claiming earlier material. It is not considered historical scripture by any major Christian tradition. The text gives the soldier the name "Longinus" in the context of the passion narrative, but without biographical elaboration.

First Visual Record: The Rabula Gospels, 586 AD

The soldier is first depicted and labelled in the Rabula Gospels — a lavishly illustrated Syriac manuscript produced in the monastery of St. John of Zagba, Mesopotamia, in 586 AD. The full-page Crucifixion miniature shows the spear-bearer with his name inscribed beside him in Syriac script. This is the earliest surviving image of the soldier with a name attached. It appears more than five centuries after the event it depicts.

Part Three

Fact Versus Tradition

A clear separation of what is recorded, what is tradition, and what is later legend.

✦ Trusted Biblical Core

What the Gospel States

An unnamed Roman soldier pierced the side of the already-dead Jesus with a spear (Greek: lonchē).

Blood and water immediately flowed from the wound — recorded as an eyewitness detail.

The act fulfilled two Hebrew prophecies (Psalm 34:20 and Zechariah 12:10).

No other Gospel records this. No canonical text names the soldier or describes his fate.

◈ Early Christian Tradition

4th–6th Century Sources

The Acts of Pilate (Gospel of Nicodemus, c. 4th century) names the soldier "Longinus" for the first time. This text is apocryphal — not accepted as canonical scripture.

The Rabula Gospels (586 AD) provide the first visual labelling of the soldier as Longinus — over five centuries after the crucifixion.

The name derives almost certainly from the Greek lonchē — a titular identifier rather than a recorded personal name.

The Roman Catholic Church commemorates Saint Longinus on March 15; the Eastern Orthodox Church on October 16. Veneration spread from the 5th century onward.

✧ Later Legend & Speculation

Medieval & Mystical Accretion

Medieval tradition holds that Longinus suffered from poor eyesight or partial blindness, and was instantly healed when the blood of Christ fell upon his eyes from the wound.

He is said to have immediately confessed Christ as the Son of God (harmonised from the Synoptic centurion's cry in Matthew 27:54 and Mark 15:39 — a different figure entirely).

Later hagiography claims he left military service, was baptised by the Apostles, retired to Cappadocia, and was martyred there — beheaded by order of Pontius Pilate.

None of these details appear in any first-century source. They are devotional traditions, spiritually meaningful to many, but historically unverifiable.

Part Four

The Legend of Conversion

✧ Later Christian Legend — Not Historical Record

Scholarly Caution:  The following is a reconstruction of the traditional Christian legend of Longinus. It is presented here as devotional narrative, not history. No first-century or near-contemporary source supports these events.

According to the tradition that grew around the unnamed soldier, Longinus was a Roman legionary of aging years, his eyesight failing — whether from disease or injury, the accounts differ. Some versions place him in the contubernium (eight-man tent unit) detailed to oversee executions on Golgotha that Friday afternoon.

When he raised the spear and thrust it into the side of the man already dead, the blood that ran down the shaft and over his hands carried — in the tradition — something more than crimson warmth. Drops reached his eyes. And in that moment, the legend says, his sight was restored.

Shaken, he is said to have uttered words drawn from the Synoptic centurion: "Truly, this was the Son of God." Here the tradition makes a conflation that scholars note — John's spear-soldier and the Synoptic centurion who makes this confession are almost certainly two different people, present in different Gospels for different purposes.

"The blood ran down the shaft onto his hands — and in that moment, the blind man saw."
— Medieval hagiographic tradition, c. 10th–13th century

In the hagiographic elaboration, Longinus abandoned his military post, sought out the Apostles in Jerusalem, received instruction and baptism, and eventually withdrew to his home region of Cappadocia (in modern Turkey). There he lived as a monk and evangelist until the local governor — acting, the legend says, on orders from Pilate — had him arrested, his teeth drawn, his tongue cut out. Even mute, tradition holds, he miraculously spoke and performed healings.

He was ultimately beheaded. The severed head, the story concludes, restored the governor's sight when he touched it in remorse — an inversion and echo of the healing at Golgotha. His relics were eventually claimed by the basilica of Sant'Andrea in Mantua, Italy, where they remain venerated to this day.
