Map I · Jerusalem, c. 30 AD

Modern Jerusalem overlaid with the approximate locations of first-century sites. Golgotha (the Place of the Skull) was a rocky outcrop just outside the city walls — the site of the Crucifixion and the piercing recorded in John 19:34. The Church of the Holy Sepulchre, built by Constantine in c. 326 AD, now covers both Golgotha and the traditional tomb site.

Map tiles © OpenStreetMap contributors. Site locations are scholarly approximations based on current archaeological consensus.

Map II · The Lance's Journey · 570–1806 AD

Numbered waypoints show the lance's documented and traditional custodial chain. Solid red lines indicate historically documented transfers; dashed gold lines show traditional or disputed routes. Click each marker for source information and reliability assessment.

Custody Waypoints

1

Jerusalem

c. 30 – 570 AD

Pilgrim of Piacenza records a lance relic in the Church of the Holy Sepulchre (c. 570).

2

Constantinople

c. 614 – 1204 AD

Byzantine imperial relic. Sacked in 1204 by the Fourth Crusade; relics dispersed to the West.

3

Antioch (Disputed)

1098 AD · Lost

First Crusade "discovery" — widely contested, lost after Peter Bartholomew's death.

4

East Francia (Germany)

c. 925 – 1424 AD

Henry I acquires lance from Rudolph II. Otto I carries it at Lechfeld. Becomes Holy Roman imperial relic.

5

Nuremberg

1424 – 1796 AD

Sigismund orders regalia permanently housed in Nuremberg. Displayed annually at the Heiltumsweisung.

6

Vienna

1796 – Present

Evacuated before Napoleon. Held at the Kunsthistorisches Museum since 1806. Briefly moved to Nuremberg 1938–1946.

Map III · First Crusade Routes · 1096–1099 AD

The primary Crusading contingents converged on Constantinople before marching south through Anatolia. The siege of Antioch (October 1097 – June 1098) was where Peter Bartholomew claimed to discover the Holy Lance beneath the Cathedral of Saint Peter. Click Antioch for the full scholarly debate entry. Jerusalem fell on July 15, 1099.

Map IV · Relic Locations · Present Day

Gold markers indicate publicly accessible relic sites. Click each for visiting information. The Vienna spear (Kunsthistorisches Museum) is the only relic to have undergone published metallurgical dating; its blade was dated to c. 7th century AD — Carolingian rather than 1st-century Roman manufacture.
